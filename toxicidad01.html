Toxicities


BASIC COURSE IN IMMUNOTHERAPY

3.1 GASTROINTESTINAL TOXICITY - ENTEROCOLITIS


Introduction


Enterocolitis which presents as diarrhoea is one of the most common immune-mediated adverse effects (imAEs).


They are described in 5-44% of patients treated with inhibitors of immune checkpoints, especially with anti-CTLA4 (Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte Antigen 4) drugs. Diarrhoea is the clinical manifestation of inflammation of the colon walls by infiltration of neutrophils and/or lymphocytes.


Early diagnosis and treatment of immune-mediated enterocolitis is essential for good clinical progress with satisfactory results. Early administration of corticosteroids can prevent deleterious and potentially life-threatening adverse effects, such as intestinal perforation.


Currently there is no premedication indicated for immune-mediated diarrhoea. It is important that the patient is well informed of the risks related to the immunotherapy drugs that are administered, as well as the importance of contacting medical personnel in the event of diarrhoeic stools (or intestinal transit disorders) from the start of treatment.


Health education is the best for the early diagnosis of enterocolitis, as well as the recording of the number and volume of stools, date and related symptoms.




Diagnosis



Increased number of normal stools and changes in the characteristics and consistency of stools in comparison to what is normal


       Nocturnal diarrhoea or faecal incontinence


       Blood or mucus in stools


       Colic-type abdominal pain, gas


       Asthenia, fever, generalised weakness and dehydration symptoms given the water-electrolyte loss.

Incidence


Associated with CTLA-4 inhibitors: 30-44% with time to onset in the first 5 weeks after the first treatment dose.


Associated with PD-1 or PLD-1 inhibitors: 5-20%.





Therapeutic management


 GRADE 1:  Increase in stool number < 4 per day; mild increase in bowel movements with respect to baseline.

Does not require discontinuation of the drug. Dietary control Increased water-electrolyte support.


Starting Loperamide 4 mg (1st dose) and after each episode of diarrhoea 2mg (up to a maximum of 16 mg daily for 3 days)


If it is not resolved after 3 days, treat as a GRADE 2.


 GRADE 2:  Increased number of stools of 4-6 faeces per day; moderate increase in bowel movements with respect to baseline.


Discontinuation of the drug. Same dietary-hygiene-therapeutic measures as for GRADE 1. Analysis of stool cultures, C. difficile and parasite toxins. Consult digestive system specialist. Consider colonoscopy +/- biopsy.


Starting corticosteroid therapy:


       Prednisolone 1 mg/kg oraly for 3 days (maximum 60 mg), reducing to 20 mg and maintain for 1-2 weeks


or


       Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg/day intravenously until GRADE 1, reduce dose and start prednisolone 1 mg/kg orally, reducing the dose over a period of 6 weeks.


If it is not resolved/improved to GRADE 1 after 3 days, treat as GRADE 3-4.


 GRADE 3:  Increase in stool number > 7 per day; faecal incontinence; indication for hospitalisation; severe increase in bowel movements with respect to baseline.


Consider permanent discontinuation of the drug. Same dietary-hygiene-therapeutic measures as for GRADE 2. Hospitalisation and water-electrolyte support via IV route.


Starting corticosteroids with Methylprednisolone 4 mg/kg/day intravenously until GRADE 1, then reduce dose and start prednisolone 1 mg/kg, reducing the dose over a period of 6 weeks.


If not resolved/improved to GRADE 1 after 3 days:


       Infliximab 5 mg/kg intravenously


       Consider Octreotide 100 µg subcutaneously, up to 200 µg subcutaneously if there is a high loss of fluid (blood volume) due to diarrhoea.


If the diarrhoea persists and/or there is risk of perforation, consider colectomy.


 GRADE 4:  Dangerous and potentially life-threatening consequences; require urgent intervention.


Same guidelines as GRADE 3.


 GRADE 5:  Death.
