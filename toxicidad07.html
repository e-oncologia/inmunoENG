3.7 NEUROLOGICAL TOXICITY


Introduction


Neurological toxicities are a rare type of immune-mediated toxicity. They have been reported in 2% of patients treated with ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4), Nivolumab and Pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1).


Melanoma is a very immunogenic tumour with the ability to present an immune response in the host.


Some studies have shown that melanoma patients present high levels of IL-6 in the cerebrospinal fluid.


High IL-6 levels produce indirect damage due to the production of nitric oxide synthase by the microglia. Some immune checkpoint inhibitors increase IL-6 expression.


The clinical manifestations described as immune-mediated neurological toxicities are the following: sensory-motor neuropathies of the Guillain-Barré syndrome type, immune-mediated encephalitis, inflammatory granulomatosis of the central nervous system, Myasthenia Grave-like syndrome, reversible posterior leukoencephalopathy, aseptic meningitis, radiculoneuropathies, transverse myelitis, and Bell's palsy.


Since the symptoms presented are so rare, attention by the attending physician as well as the patient is required in order to avoid serious adverse events.



Diagnosis



       Blurred vision, headache, nausea, vomiting, impaired gait, vertigo, speech impairment, myoclonus, seizures, loss of consciousness..


       Altered memory, dizziness, confusion, hallucinations...


       Sensory and/or motor deficit, anaesthesia, paresis, plegia...


       Cranial nerve disorder


       Nuchal rigidity


       Motor spasticity…


       Neuropathic pain, allodynia…


       Asthenia, muscle weakness, fever...

Incidence


Related to CTLA-4 or PD-1/PLD-1 inhibitors: below 1%.





Differential diagnosis



  A   Infectious causes: bacterial, viral, parasitic


  B   Other pharmacological/toxic/previous radiotherapy causes


  C   Seizures, epileptic status


  D   Ischaemic or haemorrhagic strokes


  E   Myasthenia gravis


  F   Guillain-Barré Syndrome


  G   Psychiatric disorders (conversion disorder, etc.)


  H   Causes due to direct injury or compression of the nerve root / medullary cord


   I    Metabolic causes


  J   Idiopathic / iatrogenic causes

Therapeutic management


Monitor neurological signs and symptoms, especially non-specific ones such as memory impairment, dizziness, confusion, change in character, hallucinations, seizures...


Evaluate in any degree, complete study with electromyogram and study of nerve conduction and refer a Neurology specialist to start specific symptomatic treatment of the condition.

 GRADE 1:  Asymptomatic or minimal clinical discomfort.

Does not require discontinuation of the drug.


Perform a complete clinical examination. Monitor the clinical course of the symptoms.


 GRADE 2:  Moderate symptoms that limit the instrumental daily living activities.


In the case of acute motor neurotoxicity or neuropathy, stop the treatment until resolution or improvement to GRADE 1. In case of neuropathy or neuropathic pain, consider treatment discontinuation until resolved or improved to GRADE 1.


Assessment by a neurologist.


Start Prednisolone at 0.5-1 mg/kg/day orally or equivalent intravenous dose.


In the case of neuropathic pain, add analgesics (Gabapentin, Duloxetine).


If the symptoms do not improve despite treatment, treat as GRADE 3-4.



 GRADE 3/4:  Severe symptoms which limit basic daily living activities. Risk that compromises the patient’s life.


In the case of GRADE 3: Discontinue treatment until resolution or improvement to GRADE 1. If no improvement is seen in 30 days or in case of GRADE 4, discontinue the drug permanently.


Assessment by a neurologist and complementary studies (brain MRI, lumbar puncture, nerve or muscle or skin biopsy if indicated by clinical practice).


Start Methylprednisolone 1-2 mg/kg/day intravenously (or equivalent dose)


If symptoms do not improve in 3-5 days despite treatment, assess treatment with intravenous IgG immunoglobulins or plasmapheresis, especially in cases of Guillain-Barré syndrome, and treatment with Pyridostigmine in cases of Myasthenia Gravis.


Once the clinical picture has stabilised, maintain for at least 28 days before starting a decreasing corticosteroid regimen.


 GRADE 5:  Death.
