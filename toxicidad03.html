Renal toxicity to immunotherapy?



Initial studies found a low incidence of renal toxicity associated with inhibitors at checkpoints (2-3%), but more recent data suggest a higher rate of around 13-29% (Cortazar, Kidney Int 2016, Mason JCO 2016, Hirsch J Am Soc Nephrol 2016).


The most commonly reported renal toxicities were renal failure and hyponatraemia.


Acute interstitial nephritis is the most common finding detected in biopsies.


Cortazar and colleagues analysed data from phase 2 and 3 studies with patients who had acute renal failure and found an overall incidence of 2.2% among 3,695 patients, with severity in 0.6%.


The incidence in patients treated with the combination of an anti-CTLA4 and an anti-PD1 (4.9%) was higher than in patients receiving monotherapy with either of them (1.4-2%). However, several analyses were later reported in which this incidence is greater.


Another issue that also needs to be evaluated in prospective studies is the combination of chemotherapy with immunotherapy, as it seems that the renal toxicity appears in relation to either of the two agents in monotherapy and may alter the filtration of certain agents.


The most frequent clinical manifestations of this renal toxicity are the increase in creatinine and pyuria, although there may also be haematuria, eosinophilia and worsening of arterial hypertension. Nephrotic syndrome is an rare finding that appears with the anti-CTLA4.

How do we define renal toxicity?


The grades of renal toxicity are defined according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE), Version 4.0 (CTCAE v4)


GRADE 1

Mild symptoms

Creatinine above the upper limit of normal, but <1.5 times this value

GRADE 2

Moderate symptoms

Creatinine 1.5-6 times the upper limit of normal

GRADE 3

Serious symptoms but no immediate risk to life

Creatinine 1.5-6 times the upper limit of normal

GRADE 4

Serious symptoms with life-threatening symptoms requiring urgent intervention

Creatinine > 6 times the upper limit of normal

Management of renal toxicity


Management of renal toxicity, due to immunotherapy will depend on its level of severity (Wanchoo, Am J Nephrol 2017, Champiat Ann Oncol 2016, Kumar Frontiers in Pharmacology 2017, Bristol-Myers REMS 2012, Haanen, Ann Oncol. 2017; Thompson. J Natl Compr Canc Netw. 2018; Brahmer. J Clin Oncol. 2018).


The following table summarises its management:


GRADE

Management

Follow-up

1

• Support treatment

• Continue treatment and monitor renal function and water-electrolyte balance

• If renal function worsens, treat according to the corresponding degree

2–3

• Do not administer immunotherapy

• Oral prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day or equivalent

• Monitoring of renal function and water-electrolyte balance every 2-3 days

• Consider renal biopsy

• If the symptoms are resolved and the toxicity decreases to <GRADE 1, restart immunotherapy at the next dose

• Descending corticosteroid regimen for at least one month with weekly monitoring of renal function

• If there is no response in 3 days, increase prednisone to 2 mg/kg/day and discontinue immunotherapy permanently. If it persists for 7 days, treat as GRADE 4

4

• Discontinue immunotherapy permanently

• Oral prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day or equivalent

• Monitoring of renal function and water-electrolyte balance

• Consult Nephrology

• Consider ultrasound and/or biopsy

• After the symptoms have disappeared and the impaired renal function has been resolved, perform a descending corticosteroid regimen for at least 1 month with weekly monitoring of renal function

Therapeutic decision for this patient :


• Due to the persistence of the impaired renal function, a renal biopsy was performed. Histology of tubulointerstitial nephritis was observed, attributable to Pembrolizumab.

• We started prednisone, confirming one with slow but progressive improvement until it stabilised at 1.7 times the upper limit of normal. A descending regimen of corticosteroid therapy was given until its withdrawal 2 months after it was started.

• Pembrolizumab was discontinued permanently.

• 4 months after the last administration, the patient continues to have a partial response.
