Toxicities


BASIC COURSE IN IMMUNOTHERAPY

3.6 CUTANEOUS TOXICITY


Introduction


Skin toxicities are the most common immune-mediated toxicities. They have been described in 43-45% of patients being treated with Ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4), presenting mainly in the form of rash and pruritus, followed by vitiligo.


 The rash  is usually reticular, erythematous, oedematous and maculopapular, affecting generally the trunk and extremities.


 Vitiligo  occurs in 10% of patients, being observed more frequently in patients treated with anti-PD-1 than with anti-TCLA-4, mainly affected by melanomas.


 Pruritus  usually occurs in 6-20% of patients treated with anti-PD-1 and up to 35% with anti-CTLA-4, mostly in patients with melanoma.


Severe presentation patterns such as Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis usually appear very isolated and, mainly, in cases of combined treatments with anti-PD-1 / PDL-1 and anti-CTLA-4 with an incidence of 4.8%.


Currently, there is no premedication indicated as prophylaxis for immune-mediated cutaneous toxicities.


Health education: It is important that the patient has good skin hygiene and moisturisation, as well as correct sun protection against UV rays; correct health education is considered to be fundamental.



Diagnosis



       Pruritus, mainly in the trunk and extremities.


       Erythematous and maculopapular or acneiform or psoriasis type rash. In extreme cases, bullous type rash and with displacement/removal of the dermis layer.


       Peeling and skin exfoliation.


       Vitiligo with skin pigmentation. Discolouration of the skin.


       Asthenia, fever, generalised weakness and symptoms of dehydration and infection in extreme cases.





Differential diagnosis



  A   Allergic/hypersensitivity reaction


  B   Pharmacological cause (other drugs)


  C   Cause of infection (bacteria, fungal, etc.)


  D   Photosensitivity


  E   Psoriasis


  F   Dermatitis

Therapeutic management


Monitor the signs and symptoms of dermatitis (rash and pruritus). The management of toxicities depends on the severity of the extension based on the Body Surface Area (BSA). Assessment by a dermatologist is recommended. If the patient has a bullous rash, the drug must be stopped immediately.


 GRADE 1:  Rash/pruritus localised in <10% of BSA.

Does not require discontinuation of the drug. Symptomatic treatment, including :


• Oral antipruritic agents: antihistamines such as Diphenhydramine or Hydroxyzine, and topical: based on cold compresses, compresses soaked with oat milk.


• Topical treatment with urea-based creams.


If it does not improve, treat as GRADE 2.


 GRADE 2:  Moderate rash/pruritus affecting 10-30% of BSA.


Assessment by a dermatologist.


Symptomatic treatment, including :


• Oral antipruritic agents: antihistamines such as Diphenhydramine or Hydroxyzine, and topical: based on cold compresses, compresses soaked with oat milk.


• Topical treatment with urea-based creams and betamethasone 0.1% (medium potency corticosteroid cream).


If the symptoms do not improve within 3-5 days or worsen, start Prednisolone at 0.5-1mg/kg orally, reassessing and reducing the dose over a period of 1-2 weeks. Treatment should be delayed until there is improvement to GRADE 1 or less, and with daily doses of oral prednisolone less than 10mg.

If the rash persists or recurs at GRADE 2, treatment should be stopped and a skin biopsy should be considered.


 GRADE 3/4:  Severe rash/pruritus affecting >30% of BSA.


Stop the treatment until resolution of the clinical picture to GRADE 1 or less. If, despite the treatment, there has been no clinical improvement to GRADE 1 or less within 30 days, the treatment will be permanently discontinued. In case of GRADE 4, permanent discontinuation of treatment.


Assessment by a dermatologist for taking the biopsy.


Consider hospital admission and monitor the extension of the rash.


Symptomatic treatment, such as in in GRADE 2.


Start methylprednisolone 1-2mg/kg/day intravenously (or equivalent), then reduce doses at ≥ 28 days, as the patient clinically improves. Consider prophylactic antibiotics and antifungal agents for opportunistic bacteria.


In the event of Stevens–Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis, immediate hospital admission is required as well as multidisciplinary assessment by Dermatology and the Intensive Care Unit. These patients require intravenous fluid therapy with oral and electrolyte support, antibiotic prophylaxis and permanent discontinuation of treatment.


 GRADE 5:  Death.
