3.2 PULMONARY TOXICITY


CLINICAL CASE





A 67-year-old patient, smoker and severe active alcoholic, with a history of severe chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, hypertension in medical treatment, intermittent claudication, transient ischaemic attack, hyperuricemia.


       Non-resectable cT4N0M0 p16-negative squamous cell carcinoma at the base of the tongue, treated with chemo-radiotherapy with poor tolerance and partial response


       Local progression 4 months after completion of treatment


       Nivolumab (compassionate use) 3 mg/kg every 2 weeks





After the 4th administration of the treatment, the patient attended a consultation for reassessment with computed tomography (CT). The patient reported dry and mild cough, worsening of normal dyspnoea.

• Appearance of bilateral lung infiltrates, predominantly at the right base.


• Neck MRI: partial response

Is it pulmonary toxicity to immunotherapy?



The incidence of pneumonitis varies in different clinical trials depending on the drug used and the type of cancer treated, but it is accepted that it is a rare event that occurs in less than 10% of patients (Chen, Ann Oncol 2015). However, it should be noted that it represents one of the most serious complications and may result in the death of the patient.


There is a huge variability in frequency, time of appearance, characteristics, severity, management and prognosis, so a great effort is being made to study all these aspects.


In the case of treatment with anti-CTLA4, and more specifically Ipilimumab, it is a very rare complication (<1%), but with anti-PD1 and anti-PDL1 it is somewhat more frequent.


This complication usually appears in the first 3 months in the case of anti-CTLA4 and in the first 6 in the case of anti-PD1. It tends to be resolved within around 3 months (Weber, Cancer 2013). However, these data are highly variable and can be started even after the first dose (especially in the presence of pulmonary disease) or when immunotherapy has already stopped.







Diagnosis


 Medical history 


The most common clinical symptoms are dyspnoea and non-productive cough. Other less common symptoms are fever and chest pain.


One third of the patients are asymptomatic.


It is often associated with other immunotherapy-related adverse events.


 Risk factors (Possick J, Clin Chest Med 2017) 


• Type of cancer: Non-small cell lung carcinoma has a higher incidence of pneumonitis than melanoma. Patients with haematologic malignancies and head and neck squamous cell carcinomas also have high rates; however, the sample size we have is much lower, which does not allow us to draw conclusions.


• Prior lung pathology including smoking (both smokers and ex-smokers).


• Concurrent or sequential use of anti-PD1 and anti-CTLA4, being able to reach an incidence of 10% in these cases. Prior chemotherapy or radiation therapy are not clearly associated.


• The expression of PDL1 has not been associated with an increased risk.


• It has not been associated with the treatment line.


• Response to treatment: in general, the response to treatment has not been associated with a higher rate of pneumonitis. In large cohorts of patients treated with anti-PD1 and anti-PDL1, most patients with pneumonitis have been classified as long-term responders (61%). However, the significance of this finding is not clear.

How do we define pulmonary toxicity?


The grades of pulmonary toxicity are defined according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE), Version 4.0 (CTCAE v4)



GRADE 1

Absence of symptoms

Only radiological changes

GRADE 2

Mild to moderate symptoms that limit exercise activities

GRADE 3

Severe symptoms causing patient dependence in daily living activities Hypoxia/respiratory failure

GRADE 4

Respiratory failure requiring orotracheal intubation

Management of pulmonary toxicity


The management of pulmonary toxicity due to immunotherapy will depend on its level of severity. We summarise it in the following algorhythm (Naidoo, JCO, 2016; Champiat Ann Oncol 2016, Kumar Frontiers in Pharmacology 2017):



GRADE

Management

Follow-up

1

• Do not administer immunotherapy for 2-4 weeks

• Monitor symptoms every 3 days

• If it worsens, treat according to the corresponding grade

• Very strict radiological control (each cycle)

2

• Do not administer immunotherapy

• Oral prednisone 1 mg/kg/day or equivalent

• Monitoring daily/twice a week of symptoms and chest X-ray every 1-3 days

• Perform sputum culture to rule out microbiological cause and consider fibre-optic bronchoscopy and Pulmonology consultation

• If the symptoms resolve within 72 hours, immunotherapy may be resumed. If not resolved, discontinue it completely

• In GRADE 2 recurrent pneumonitis, discontinue immunotherapy permanently

• Descending corticosteroid regimen for at least one month

3-4

• Discontinue immunotherapy permanently

• IV Methylprednisolone 2-4 mg/kg/day or equivalent

• Consider prophylactic antibiotics

• Rule out other causes, especially infectious diseases. Perform fibre-optic bronchoscopy and consult with Pulmonology

• After the disappearance of the symptoms, perform descending corticosteroid regimen for at least 1 month

• If there is no response within 48 hours, consider adding an immunosuppressant (Cyclophosphamide or infliximab)
